Why This Report Exists

AI regulation is no longer theoretical. As of February 2026, six jurisdictions have AI laws already in effect, four more are enacted with imminent deadlines, and four additional frameworks are proposed or voluntary. Yet there has been no comprehensive analysis of how the AI agent ecosystem actually stacks up against this regulatory wave.

Nerq is in a unique position to provide that analysis. We crawl GitHub, npm, PyPI, HuggingFace, and MCP registries daily, indexing every AI agent we can find. Our compliance engine classifies each agent against 14 jurisdictions automatically. This report is the first public look at the data.

The Jurisdiction Divergence Problem

One of the most significant findings is the degree of regulatory divergence across jurisdictions. The same AI system can be classified completely differently depending on which country's law you apply.

A CV screening tool is classified as high-risk in 4 out of 8 jurisdictions — but minimal-risk in the other 4. There is no single global standard for AI compliance.

Here's how a typical CV screening tool (one that automatically ranks and filters job applicants) is classified across jurisdictions:

A US company must navigate different requirements in Colorado (algorithmic discrimination), Illinois (video interview specific), and California (only frontier models).

The Compliance Cliff

Multiple deadlines are converging in the next six months:

  • Colorado AI Act — June 30, 2026 (~131 days). All high-risk AI used in consequential decisions must comply.
  • EU AI Act Annex III — August 2, 2026 (~164 days). High-risk AI systems must have conformity assessments, risk management, documentation, and human oversight.
  • California SB 942 — August 2, 2026 (~164 days). AI transparency requirements take effect.

Organizations with AI systems classified as high-risk in any of these jurisdictions should be actively working on compliance programs now. The EU AI Act alone requires conformity assessments, risk management systems, data governance procedures, technical documentation, human oversight mechanisms, and registration in the EU database — none of which can be implemented overnight.

Methodology

Nerq indexes AI agents from GitHub, npm, PyPI, HuggingFace, and MCP server registries via daily automated crawls. Classification uses a hybrid engine combining rule-based matching with LLM-enhanced analysis. Six jurisdictions have native classifiers with jurisdiction-specific logic; eight use approximate classifiers that default to minimal risk with disclosure.

Compliance scoring (0-100) is generated from gap analysis across requirement areas including risk management, data governance, human oversight, transparency, and documentation. All results should be treated as screening indicators, not definitive legal assessments.
